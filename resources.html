Definitions

Term Definition
American Stock Exchange (AMEX) The former name of NYSE MKT. The third largest stock exchange in the U.S.
Bearish A sentiment which suggests non-confidence in an increased valuation of a stock
Bollinger Band A technical indicator which is a measure of volatility of a stock
Bollinger Band Squeeze Indicates a period of low volatility and a potential good entry point
Brokerage A person or application that allows you to trade at an exchange
Bullish A sentiment which suggests confidence in an increased valuation of a stock
Capital The amount of money you have to put into the stock market
Debt The amount of money a company owes
Dividend Money that a company will pay its shareholders per stock that they own for owning it
Dow Jones Industrial Average One of the oldest stock market indexes - a measure of stock market performance.
Due Diligence (DD) The act of researching a stock to determine future actions with it
E Trade A brokerage account (see our brokerage section)
Exchange-Traded Fund (ETFs) A security that follows an index or index fund; they are traded at exchanges like stocks
Entry Point The ideal price to buy into a stock
Equity The financial value of a share of a stock that you own
Exchange The market where stock activity occurs
Exit Point The ideal price to sell a stock
Exponential Moving Average (EMA)
Food and Drug Administration (FDA) A government agency to which approves biotech companies and their drugs/devices in the United States
Futures
Fundamental Analysis Research conducted into a company's financial structure and services/product
Fundamental Trading Trading based on fundamental analysis
Futures
Indicator A sign that a stock is going to increase or decrease (oftentimes based on previous data)
Investing The act of buying a stock and holding it over time to increase its valuation
Level II (Level 2, L2) The "backpage" of a stock - see, in real time, what traders are buying and selling the stock for
Leverage Using borrowed capital so as to get a greater return from a trade or investment; riskier than a normal trade because you are trading on borrowed money
Liquidity Defined as the ability of a stock to be traded with little affect on the price of the stock
Moving Average Convergence Divergence (MACD)
National Association of Securities Dealers Automated Quotations (NASDAQ) The second biggest stock exchange
New York Stock Exchange (NYSE) The biggest international stock exchange
Options
Over the Counter (OTC) Stocks/Pink Sheet Stocks Stocks sold on non-major exchanges - typically penny or sub-penny stocks. Highly volatile and prone to manipulation
Pattern Day Trading (PTD) Buying and selling a stock on the same day more than 3 times over the period of 5 business days; only applies to those with less than $25,000 in their account
Penny Stocks Stocks under $2; oftentimes very volatile and prone to manipulation
Relative Strength Index (RSI) An indication of a whether a stock is oversold or overbought
Robinhood A brokerage app for the iPhone and Android
Security Any "financial instrument" (such as a stock or bond) that can be quickly exhcnaged for money
Shorts/Shorting "Betting against a stock", or that a stock is going to fall
Simple Moving Average (SMA)
Swing Trading Buying a stock and selling it a few days afterwards
Technical Analysis Analyzing stock market trends and attempting to predict what trends a stock will follow based on those trends alone
Technical Trading Trading on technical analysis
Tickers The name a company displays on the stock market
Trading The act of buying and selling shares or equity
Warrant
Volume The number of shares of a stock that are being bought and sold; high volume indicates higher volatility
